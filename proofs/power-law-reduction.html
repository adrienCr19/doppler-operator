Lemma L1 — power-law reduction

Statement

(i) On power-law spectra, \[ {}^{d}\Bo^m_{\ell\ell'}(\nu,\beta)\,\nu^{q} \;=\; {}^{d-q}\Kk^m_{\ell\ell'}(-\beta)\;\nu^{q}, \qquad \Do^m_{\ell\ell'\ell''}(\nu,\beta)\,\nu^{q} \;=\; \frac{1}{\gamma}\; {}^{-1-q}\Kk^m_{\ell\ell'}(+\beta)\;\, {}^{-q}\Kk^m_{\ell'\ell''}(-\beta)\;\nu^{q}. \] (ii) An identity between finite combinations of boost/Doppler operators (with coefficients that are polynomials in \(\Oh\)) holds as an operator identity iff the corresponding scalar-weight kernel identity holds for all real \(q\); and it suffices to check it for infinitely many values of \(q\) at each order in \(\beta\).

Proof of (i)

The energy-shift generator \(\Oh = -\nu\partial_\nu\) acts on power laws as \(\Oh\,\nu^q = -q\,\nu^q\); hence for any (formal) function \(f\), \(f(\Oh)\,\nu^q = f(-q)\,\nu^q\), because \(f(\Oh)\) is defined through its action \(e^{a\Oh} n(\nu) = n(e^{-a}\nu)\), which on \(\nu^q\) gives \(e^{-aq}\nu^q\). The boost operator is the kernel at operator weight (identity B1, [CR25, Eqs. 3, 12]): \({}^{d}\Bo^m_{\ell\ell'}(\nu,\beta) = {}^{d+\Oh}\Kk^m_{\ell\ell'}(-\beta)\), where the kernel depends on its weight analytically (the weight enters only through \([\gamma(1+\beta\mu')]^{-w}\) inside a convergent integral). Substituting \(\Oh \to -q\) proves the first relation.

For the Doppler operator, apply the two factors in order. First, \({}^{0}\Bo^m_{\ell'\ell''}(\nu,\beta)\,\nu^q = {}^{-q}\Kk^m_{\ell'\ell''}(-\beta)\,\nu^q\); the result is a number times \(\nu^q\). Then \({}^{-1}\Bo^m_{\ell\ell'}(\nu,-\beta) = {}^{-1+\Oh}\Kk^m_{\ell\ell'}(+\beta)\) (the sign flip \(\beta\to-\beta\) in B1 flips the kernel argument), acting on \(\nu^q\) gives \({}^{-1-q}\Kk^m_{\ell\ell'}(+\beta)\,\nu^q\). Multiplying and inserting the \(1/\gamma\) of the definition completes (i). \(\blacksquare\)

Proof of (ii)

At any fixed order \(N\) in \(\beta\), each boost operator is a polynomial in \(\Oh\) of degree \(\le N\) [CR25, §2.2]: in the series representation (B2) only \(k \le N\) frequency derivatives survive at order \(\beta^N\). A finite combination of such operators (with polynomial-in-\(\Oh\) coefficients — note all such objects commute, being functions of the single operator \(\Oh\)) is again a polynomial in \(\Oh\) at each order in \(\beta\). Two polynomials that agree at infinitely many points \(-q\) are identical; hence coefficient-wise equality as polynomials in \(\Oh\), order by order in \(\beta\), i.e. an operator identity. The converse is immediate by evaluation on \(\nu^q\). \(\blacksquare\)

Why this matters. Every derivation on this site can be carried out with ordinary functions of the real variable \(q\) in place of operator-valued Doppler weights, and every numerical verification (quadrature at a handful of non-integer \(q\)) certifies the corresponding operator identity order by order in \(\beta\). Non-integer \(q\) values are used in the harness precisely so that no accidental integer-weight degeneracies (e.g. poles of \((1-d)^{-1}\) in seed formulas) are hit.

Verification

Implicit in every check of verification/01_conventions.wl and 02_doppler.wl; in particular check C11 (closed form of \(\Do_{000}\) from [CR26, Eq. 10a], PASS at \(2\times10^{-29}\)) evaluates both sides on \(\nu^q\) for \(q \in \{-0.7, 0.5, 1.3, 2\}\).
